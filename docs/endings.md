# Best Idle Game Ever 2 — Endings

## Purpose of this document

This document describes the ending system.

Endings are one of the most important progression systems in the game.

Unlike many strategy games, endings are not merely cutscenes.

An ending is a final engineering project.

The player wins because they built a solution.

Not because a timer ended.

Not because a boss died.

Not because a score threshold was reached.

---

# Core Philosophy

The central question of the game is:

> How can civilization become safe again?

Every ending represents a different answer.

Each vector proposes its own philosophy.

---

# Design Goals

Endings should:

- emerge naturally from gameplay;
- reward specialization;
- reward experimentation;
- require meaningful preparation;
- feel mechanically distinct;
- feel narratively distinct;
- act as final puzzles.

---

# Ending Structure

Every ending follows the same broad pattern.

1. Build required infrastructure.
2. Unlock megaproject.
3. Complete megaproject.
4. Survive final challenge.
5. Win.

The differences lie in:

- requirements;
- city design;
- tower design;
- research paths;
- strategic priorities.

---

# Why Endings Are Megaprojects

A megaproject solves multiple problems.

It:

- uses existing systems;
- creates clear goals;
- scales naturally;
- fits the setting;
- avoids arbitrary victory conditions.

The player wins by building something extraordinary.

---

# Pure Endings

Every vector should have at least two major endings.

One generally represents:

Acceptance

The vector embraces the world.

The other represents:

Rejection

The vector escapes, avoids or transcends the world.

# Ending Requirements Philosophy

Endings should not merely require resources.

They should require:

- city layouts;
- multistructures;
- containment levels;
- specific technologies;
- strategic commitments.

---

# Why This Matters

Otherwise endings become:

"Collect 1,000,000 resource."

That is boring.

The player should feel:

> I built something unique.

---

# Ending Diversity

Two players reaching different endings should have visibly different cities.

Examples:

Ascension City

- mana everywhere;
- one gigantic tower.

Empire City

- huge population;
- administrative districts.

Harmony City

- almost no threat;
- ecological infrastructure.

Ark City

- industrial launch complex.

The city should tell the story.

---

# Endings as Build Validation

An ending is not separate from gameplay.

It is the ultimate proof that a strategy worked.

The player should look at the completed city and think:

> Of course this city reached this ending.

---

# Future Expansion

Additional endings can be added later through:

- new vectors;
- new megastructures;
- new regions;
- hybrid combinations.

The framework should remain extensible.

---

# Design Rules

Whenever a new ending is proposed:

1. Does it express a philosophy?
2. Does it require unique infrastructure?
3. Does it create a different city?
4. Does it use existing systems?
5. Does it feel like a solution to the safety problem?

If not, it is probably not an ending.

---

# Design North Star

The game begins with:

> How do we survive?

The game ends with:

> What does survival ultimately mean?
