# Best Idle Game Ever 2 — Migration, Colonization and Meta Progression

## Purpose of this document

This document describes the long-term progression systems of the game.

The game is not intended to revolve around a single city.

Cities are temporary.

The world is permanent.

The player's story is the story of many settlements.

Migration and colonization exist to create long-term progression without relying on traditional prestige currencies.

---

# Core Philosophy

Most games solve progression through one of two systems.

---

## Permanent City

The player stays forever.

Examples:

- SimCity
- Cities Skylines

Problem:

The city eventually becomes solved.

---

## Prestige Reset

The player resets everything.

Examples:

- Cookie Clicker
- Idle Wizard
- many mobile idle games

Problem:

Often feels artificial.

---

# This Project's Approach

Cities are temporary.

Knowledge persists.

Infrastructure history persists.

The player builds a civilization through multiple cities.

Not through repeated resets.

---

# Design Goals

Migration should:

- create meaningful long-term decisions;
- avoid fake prestige currencies;
- support multiple playstyles;
- create attachment to cities;
- create reasons to explore new regions;
- make the world feel larger than a single settlement.

---

# Why Migrate

Migration may happen for many reasons.

Examples:

- region exhausted;
- better location discovered;
- threat becoming difficult to manage;
- demolition footprint accumulating too much signature;
- old one-use buildings clogging valuable city space;
- specific ending requirements;
- specialist accumulation strategy.

Migration should feel like an opportunity.

Not a punishment.

---

# Demolition Pressure

Rebuilding a city creates permanent footprint.

When buildings are demolished, the city clears internal space but pushes rubble, trash, ritual residue, broken machines, contaminated biomass, and other waste outside the walls.

This permanently increases that city's signature/Threat.

The player therefore faces a long-term settlement question:

- keep obsolete early buildings and lose usable land;
- demolish them and make the city easier for monsters to find;
- migrate and start a cleaner settlement elsewhere.

This gives migration a practical role beyond prestige.

A mature city can become powerful but dirty: full of efficient districts, old foundations, external dumps, and accumulated attention.

A new city is smaller but cleaner.

---

# Openness and New Sites

Every potential settlement site has Openness.

Openness determines:

- maximum possible city size;
- visibility/Threat multiplier.

Hidden terrain supports smaller, safer cities.

Open terrain supports larger, more visible cities.

This makes migration site choice strategic.

Early migrations may search for concealed places that reduce pressure.

Late migrations may deliberately choose open locations because the civilization has the technology, camouflage, logistics, and containment needed to build a much larger city there.

---

# Two Strategic Paths

The game supports two primary approaches.

---

# Path 1 — Nomad

Build.
Grow.
Leave.

The city is a tool.

Knowledge is the goal.

---

# Path 2 — Colonizer

Build.
Grow.
Preserve.

The city is an asset.

The empire is the goal.

---

# Nomad Philosophy

Nomads focus on expertise.

Every city teaches something.

The settlement itself is temporary.

The people are what matters.

---

# Specialists

When abandoning a city:

The player extracts specialists.

Specialists represent:

- master engineers;
- scholars;
- farmers;
- artificers;
- commanders;
- architects.

They carry knowledge forward.

---

# Specialist Formula

Current proposal:

+1% Support Bonus

for every

100 Produced Support

generated by the abandoned city.

Produced Support matters.

Not Free Support.

---

# Why Produced Support

Example:

City A

Produces:
1000

Consumes:
950

Free:
50

This city should still generate many specialists.

It is productive.

Using Free Support would incorrectly reward inefficient behavior.

---

# Specialist Accumulation

Bonuses stack permanently.

Example:

City 1:
+5%

City 2:
+7%

City 3:
+9%

Total:

+21%

This creates a long-term growth curve.

---

# Why Specialists Exist

Specialists solve several problems.

They:

- reward successful cities;
- encourage migration;
- avoid prestige currencies;
- fit the setting.

The player carries expertise, not abstract points.

---

# Nomad Endgame

A dedicated Nomad eventually creates:

A city built by centuries of accumulated masters.

Few settlements.

Extremely efficient.

Extremely advanced.

---

# Colonizer Philosophy

Colonizers preserve settlements.

Every city remains useful.

The player gradually creates an empire.

---

# Colony Definition

A colony is any city that remains active after the player leaves.

Colonies continue operating.

They generate support.

They contribute to larger goals.

---

# Why Colonies Exist

Without colonies:

Old cities become irrelevant.

With colonies:

Old cities remain valuable.

The player's history matters.

---

# Capital

One city may be designated as the Capital.

The Capital becomes the center of the empire.

---

# Capital Functions

The Capital:

- receives imports;
- hosts megaprojects;
- unlocks empire-scale structures;
- becomes a major support sink.

---

# Why Only One Capital

Multiple capitals create complexity.

One capital creates identity.

The player always knows:

> This is the center of my civilization.

---

# Support Imports

Colonies may transfer support to the Capital.

Example:

Free Electricity:
100

Transfer Rate:
25%

Capital receives:
25

Colony keeps:
75

---

# Why Imports Use Free Support

Using Produced Support creates bookkeeping problems.

Example:

City A supports City B

City B supports City C

City C supports City A

Disaster.

Using Free Support avoids recursive chains.

---

# Capital Deficits

The Capital may become economically unhealthy.

This is allowed.

Example:

Imported Support disappears.

Capital enters deficit.

Existing systems continue operating.

New construction becomes impossible.

---

# Why Deficits Are Allowed

Creates interesting stories.

Examples:

- ancient capital;
- declining empire;
- overextended civilization.

Recovery becomes gameplay.

---

# Capital Megaprojects

Some structures may only exist in the Capital.

Examples:

- Imperial Palace;
- World Congress;
- Planetary Archive;
- Great Academy;
- Ascension Nexus.

This gives Capitals a unique role.

---

# Capital Transfer

The player may move the Capital.

But it should not be easy.

---

# Transfer Requirements

Current proposal:

- positive support balance;
- no critical imports;
- minimum infrastructure level.

This prevents exploitative relocation.

---

# Why Capital Transfer Exists

Sometimes:

- geography changes;
- new region is superior;
- old capital declines.

The player should not be trapped forever.

---

# Colony Specialization

Different colonies may serve different roles.

Examples:

Industrial Colony

Produces Electricity.

Research Colony

Produces Knowledge.

Nature Sanctuary

Produces Biomass.

Military Colony

Supports defenses.

---

# Empire Layout

A mature empire may look like:

Capital

↓

Industrial Colony

↓

Research Colony

↓

Agricultural Colony

↓

Arcane Colony

Each city has identity.

---

# Migration and Regions

Migration is strongly connected to regions.

Different regions provide:

- different opportunities;
- different crises;
- different enemies.

The player is encouraged to explore.

---

# Why Exploration Matters

Without exploration:

The optimal city becomes permanent.

With exploration:

The player constantly discovers new possibilities.

---

# Abandoned Cities

Some cities are intentionally abandoned.

These cities become part of history.

Their support disappears.

Their specialist bonus remains.

---

# Why Abandonment Matters

Players should not feel obligated to manage infinite settlements.

Abandonment keeps the game manageable.

---

# Colonizer vs Nomad Balance

Neither path should dominate.

Nomad Advantages:

- simpler management;
- stronger specialist bonuses;
- faster adaptation.

Colonizer Advantages:

- empire-scale projects;
- larger total support;
- persistent infrastructure.

---

# Hybrid Play

Many players will combine both styles.

Example:

Keep valuable colonies.

Abandon mediocre cities.

This should be fully supported.

---

# World History

Every city contributes to the player's history.

The world should feel lived in.

The player should remember:

- first settlement;
- first capital;
- first megaproject;
- failed regions;
- abandoned cities.

---

# Future Extensions

Possible future systems:

- trade routes;
- colony governors;
- regional autonomy;
- civil wars;
- migration events;
- inter-city specialization.

The migration framework should support expansion.

---

# Migration and Endings

Different endings favor different approaches.

Examples:

Hidden Village

Strong Nomad synergy.

Empire

Strong Colonizer synergy.

Ascension

Either path.

Planetary Restoration

Usually favors large networks.

---

# Design Rules

Whenever a new migration mechanic is proposed:

1. Does it support Nomads?
2. Does it support Colonizers?
3. Does it create meaningful decisions?
4. Does it avoid bookkeeping nightmares?
5. Does it fit the world narrative?

If not, reconsider the mechanic.

---

# Design North Star

Cities are temporary.

Civilization is permanent.

The player is not building a settlement.

The player is building a history.
