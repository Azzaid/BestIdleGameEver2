# Best Idle Game Ever 2 — Regions and Crises

## Purpose of this document

This document describes:

- regions;
- environmental modifiers;
- enemy ecosystems;
- crises;
- cross-vector interactions.

Regions prevent the game from becoming solved permanently.

Crises prevent the player from focusing exclusively on one vector.

Together they create adaptation pressure.

---

# Core Philosophy

A city should not exist in a vacuum.

The world matters.

The same build should not be equally effective everywhere.

The player should regularly ask:

> Is this still the right city for this place?

---

# Design Goals

Regions should:

- create variety;
- encourage experimentation;
- change priorities;
- influence tower design;
- influence city design;
- create reasons to migrate.

Crises should:

- punish excessive specialization;
- reward hybrid solutions;
- create new planning challenges;
- remain manageable.

---

# Regions

A region is a temporary settlement location.

The player does not own the entire world.

They occupy one region at a time.

Eventually:

- expand;
- migrate;
- colonize;
- abandon.

---

# Region Structure

Every region consists of:

Terrain Modifiers
+
Enemy Ecosystem
+
Unique Opportunities
+
Unique Problems

---

# Terrain Modifiers

Terrain modifiers affect city building.

Examples:

- biomass production;
- mana efficiency;
- building placement;
- threat generation;
- support output.

---

# Example: Dry Region

Description:

A harsh and arid environment.

Effects:

- Biomass Production -25%
- Mana Storage +20%
- Threat from Nature Buildings +10%

Implication:

Nature struggles.

Aether thrives.

---

# Example: Mountain Region

Effects:

- Farmland Space -30%
- Technology Buildings +15%
- Defensive Structures +20%

Implication:

Industrial fortress.

Limited agriculture.

---

# Example: Ancient Arcane Scar

Effects:

- Mana Production +50%
- Arcane Threat +50%
- Reality Instability events enabled

Implication:

Very powerful.

Very dangerous.

---

# Example: Living Forest

Effects:

- Nature Buildings +30%
- Industrial Buildings +25% Threat
- Beast-type enemies stronger

Implication:

Supports biological cities.

Punishes industry.

---

# Example: Ruined Megacity

Effects:

- Technology Production +40%
- Free Infrastructure
- Machine Enemies

Implication:

Fast start.

Dangerous late game.

---

# Why Regions Exist

Without regions:

Optimal city found once.

Game solved forever.

With regions:

Every new location changes priorities.

---

# Enemy Ecosystems

Enemy composition is tied to regions.

Not Threat.

Not city size.

Not progression.

Region.

---

# Forest Ecosystem

Typical enemies:

- beasts;
- insects;
- predators;
- living plants.

Weaknesses:

Area damage.

Strong control.

---

# Industrial Ecosystem

Typical enemies:

- drones;
- machines;
- war constructs.

Weaknesses:

Armor penetration.

EMP effects.

---

# Arcane Ecosystem

Typical enemies:

- spirits;
- elementals;
- magical constructs.

Weaknesses:

Specialized targeting.

Resistance management.

---

# Mutated Ecosystem

Typical enemies:

- aberrations;
- bio-weapons;
- engineered predators.

Weaknesses:

Debuffs.

Disease.

Long fights.

---

# Threat and Ecosystems

Threat affects:

- quantity;
- strength;
- rarity.

Threat does NOT determine ecosystem.

This preserves regional identity.

---

# Rare Visitors

At high Threat levels:

additional enemies may appear.

Examples:

- dragons;
- orbital horrors;
- ancient titans;
- forgotten gods.

Lore:

The city becomes visible from farther away.

---

# Regional Opportunities

Regions should not only create problems.

They should also create opportunities.

Examples:

Volcanic Region

Unique Forge District.

Arcane Scar

Unique Ritual Complex.

Living Forest

World Grove Multistructure.

Ruined Megacity

Ancient Data Repository.

---

# Why Opportunities Matter

Players should feel excited.

Not punished.

Migration should feel like exploration.

---

# Crisis Philosophy

Crises are not disasters.

Crises are consequences.

The city created them.

---

# Design Goal of Crises

The goal is NOT:

"Stop player from specializing."

The goal IS:

"Encourage player to diversify."

This is a critical distinction.

---

# Cross-Vector Principle

Most crises should primarily hurt OTHER vectors.

This creates interesting decisions.

---

# Example

Technology causes pollution.

Pollution hurts People.

Not Technology.

This encourages:

Technology
+
Nature

instead of

More Technology.

---

# Technology Crises

Technology produces:

- pollution;
- noise;
- infrastructure strain;
- automation problems.

---

## Pollution

Triggered by:

Too many industrial buildings.

Effects:

- People Production Down
- Nature Efficiency Down

Solutions:

- forests;
- purification systems;
- ecological districts.

---

## Grid Instability

Triggered by:

Huge electrical network.

Effects:

- Arcane Buildings less effective.

Solutions:

- energy relays;
- magical stabilizers.

---

## AI Drift

Triggered by:

Extreme computing.

Effects:

- random support fluctuations.

Solutions:

- human administration;
- arcane oversight.

---

# Medieval Crises

Human systems create:

- corruption;
- bureaucracy;
- unrest.

---

## Bureaucratic Overload

Triggered by:

Huge population.

Effects:

- Technology Research Down.

Solutions:

- automation;
- computing.

---

## Social Unrest

Triggered by:

Poor city balance.

Effects:

- support losses.

Solutions:

- entertainment;
- religion;
- ecology.

---

# Nature Crises

Nature creates:

- overgrowth;
- competition;
- ecosystem collapse.

---

## Aggressive Overgrowth

Triggered by:

Too much biomass.

Effects:

- Industrial Production Down.

Solutions:

- controlled burns;
- machinery.

---

## Predator Bloom

Triggered by:

Large biological districts.

Effects:

- stronger beast enemies.

Solutions:

- military districts;
- magical wards.

---

# Aether Crises

Magic creates instability.

---

## Reality Distortion

Triggered by:

Large mana concentrations.

Effects:

- random building penalties.

Solutions:

- technology stabilizers;
- rituals.

---

## Mana Storms

Triggered by:

Arcane megastructures.

Effects:

- support fluctuations.

Solutions:

- grounding pylons;
- world anchors.

---

# Hybrid Crisis Solutions

Many crises should have multiple answers.

Example:

Pollution

Can be solved by:

Nature
or
Magic
or
Technology

The player chooses.

---

# Crisis Escalation

Crises should have levels.

Example:

Pollution I

Minor penalties.

Pollution II

Major penalties.

Pollution III

Severe penalties.

This creates time to react.

---

# Why Crises Should Escalate

Immediate punishment feels unfair.

Escalation creates planning opportunities.

---

# Regional Crises

Some crises only exist in certain regions.

Example:

Arcane Scar

Reality Fractures.

Living Forest

Predator Migration.

Megacity Ruins

Machine Awakening.

This increases replayability.

---

# Crisis and Migration

Migration is always a valid answer.

The player may decide:

"This place is becoming too expensive."

Move.

Not every problem must be solved permanently.

---

# Crisis and Endings

Different endings solve crises differently.

Example:

Hidden Village

Avoids most crises.

Empire

Absorbs crises through scale.

Ascension

Escapes crises.

World Restoration

Eliminates root causes.

---

# Future Extensions

Possible additions:

- weather systems;
- seasonal cycles;
- regional politics;
- wandering factions;
- ecosystem evolution.

The system should remain extensible.

---

# Design Rules

Whenever a new region is added:

1. What does it reward?
2. What does it punish?
3. What enemies live there?
4. What unique structures exist there?

Whenever a new crisis is added:

1. What caused it?
2. Who suffers?
3. How can it be solved?
4. Does it encourage synergy?

If those questions are unclear, the design is probably incomplete.

---

# Design North Star

Regions create variety.

Crises create adaptation.

Together they ensure that no city design remains optimal forever.
