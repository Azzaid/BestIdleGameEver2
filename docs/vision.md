no# Best Idle Game Ever 2 — Vision

## Purpose of this document

This file defines the high-level vision of the game: what it is, what emotional fantasy it should create, and which design principles should guide all later mechanics.

It is not a technical specification.  
It is a compass.

---

# High Concept

**Best Idle Game Ever 2** is a hybrid of:

- city builder;
- tower defense;
- weapon-construction game;
- long-form progression puzzle.

The player controls a group of almost immortal survivors from the old world. They travel through a ruined techno-magical-biological planet, found temporary cities, build defenses, research lost technologies, and slowly search for a place where people can finally live without constant danger.

The game is not about conquering the world immediately.

It is about repeatedly asking:

> Can we make this place safe enough to live here?

---

# Core Fantasy

The player is not merely placing towers.

The player is building a fragile civilization in a hostile world.

Every city is both:

- a shelter;
- a machine;
- a signal fire;
- a provocation.

The more advanced the city becomes, the more visible it is to the world around it.

Progress is not free.  
Every step forward attracts attention.

---

# Central Gameplay Statement

The entire game revolves around this loop:

> The city creates support.  
> Support enables buildings, research, walls, and towers.  
> Growth increases threat.  
> Threat summons attacks.  
> Attacks test whether the city deserves to grow further.

This should remain true at every scale:

- early village;
- industrial city;
- magical fortress;
- biological sanctuary;
- imperial capital;
- final world-repair project.

---

# Main Design Pillars

## 1. Preparation matters more than combat

Combat is the climax, not the whole game.

The player spends most of the time:

- building the city;
- arranging hexes;
- combining structures;
- designing towers;
- choosing research paths;
- preparing for the next stress test.

Then combat begins.

The fight should feel like the moment when the player's design is finally tested.

The ideal feeling:

> I built this system. Now let’s see if it survives.

---

## 2. No classic idle resource hoarding

The game should avoid the common idle pattern:

1. Wait.
2. Accumulate currency.
3. Buy bigger number.
4. Repeat.

Instead, the central economy is based on **support / upkeep**.

The player does not wait for a treasury to fill.

The player increases the city's ability to sustain complexity.

This means:

- no meaningful offline farming;
- no infinite stockpiles;
- no reason to leave the game alone for a week and return rich.

Progress comes from changing the structure of the city.

---

## 3. The city is a puzzle

The city should not be a flat list of buildings.

It should be a spatial system.

Buildings:

- produce support;
- consume support;
- increase threat;
- reduce threat;
- strengthen neighbors;
- form multistructures;
- unlock research;
- create crises.

The player should care where buildings are placed.

A city should feel like a living puzzle-machine.

---

## 4. Synergy is the heart of the game

The game has four development vectors:

- Technology;
- Medieval / Human;
- Aether / Magic;
- Nature / Biology.

Each vector has strong advantages and clear weaknesses.

The interesting part is not choosing one forever.

The interesting part is discovering how they interact.

Examples:

- Technology gives precision to unstable medieval weapons.
- Nature slows enemies so slow magic can hit.
- Medieval systems provide flexible targeting logic early.
- Aether adds impossible global effects.
- Biology can reduce threat and control crowds without killing them.

The player should constantly ask:

> What happens if I combine these two things?

---

## 5. Broken builds are allowed

The game should not be over-balanced into blandness.

It is acceptable if the player creates:

- a city with weak towers and absurd walls;
- one gigantic god-tower;
- a hidden village with almost no threat;
- a polluted industrial monster-city;
- a magic city that barely exists in normal space;
- a nomadic chain of master-builders;
- an old capital surviving on imported support.

The player should feel clever for finding extreme solutions.

The design goal is not perfect competitive balance.

The design goal is expressive systems.

---

## 6. The wall connects everything

The wall is the conceptual and visual axis of the game.

It separates:

- city from wilderness;
- planning from combat;
- civilization from monsters;
- safety from danger.

But it also connects those things.

In the city view, the wall is part of the hex grid.

In combat, the same wall appears at the bottom of the battlefield.

The player should understand:

> This battle is not a separate mode.  
> This is my city being tested from the outside.

---

# World Tone

The world is post-catastrophic, but not empty.

It is not a grey wasteland.

The old civilization used:

- advanced machines;
- biological engineering;
- magic;
- human institutions.

After the collapse, the world became wildly diverse.

Possible remnants:

- cybernetic beasts;
- autonomous war machines;
- magical anomalies;
- forests grown from engineered biomass;
- mutant swarms;
- ruined imperial cities;
- old orbital debris;
- broken god-machines;
- feral spell constructs.

The apocalypse created abundance, not emptiness.

The world is dangerous because it is overflowing with uncontrolled systems.

---

# The Survivors

The player faction is a group of people from the old world.

They may be:

- immortal;
- heavily augmented;
- magically preserved;
- biologically modified;
- too stubborn to die.

They remember enough to rebuild.

But they cannot simply recreate the old civilization.

The world has changed too much.

Their long-term goal is simple and almost humble:

> Find a place where people can live safely.

This goal can be achieved in different ways:

- by hiding;
- by conquering;
- by leaving the planet;
- by phasing out of reality;
- by becoming part of nature;
- by repairing the world itself.

---

# Intended Player Experience

The game should appeal to players who enjoy:

- system-building;
- automation-like thinking;
- build crafting;
- city planning;
- tower customization;
- emergent combinations;
- long-term progression;
- experimenting with strange strategies.

The player should feel:

- clever when a build works;
- responsible when the city becomes too visible;
- curious about new regions;
- tempted by risky upgrades;
- attached to old cities;
- excited when a multistructure forms;
- satisfied when a battle validates preparation.

---

# What the game is not

The game is not meant to be:

- a fast action tower defense;
- a clicker based on waiting;
- a perfectly balanced competitive game;
- a pure city builder with cosmetic combat;
- a puzzle game with one correct solution;
- a mobile-style prestige loop with abstract currencies.

It should feel like a deep sandbox of interacting systems.

---

# Core Tension

Every major decision should touch at least two sides of this triangle:

## Growth

More land.  
More buildings.  
More research.  
More towers.  
More possibilities.

## Safety

Lower threat.  
Stronger wall.  
Better containment.  
Stable city.

## Visibility

Bigger structures.  
Louder machines.  
Stronger magic.  
More monsters.

The best decisions are rarely free.

---

# Victory Philosophy

Victory is not one universal goal.

Victory is the moment when the city finally solves the problem of safety.

Different solutions should feel mechanically and narratively different.

Examples:

- Technology escapes time or the planet.
- Nature integrates with the world.
- Aether changes the city's phase of existence.
- Humanity creates an empire or disappears into a hidden village.
- All vectors together repair the planet.

Endings are not just cutscenes.

They are final megaprojects.

The player builds their ending.

---

# Long-Term Structure

The game may contain many cities over time.

Some are abandoned.

Some become colonies.

One may become a capital.

A player can lean toward:

- **Nomad** — abandon cities, gather specialists, concentrate mastery.
- **Colonizer** — preserve cities, create an empire, feed the capital.

Both should be valid.

The world becomes a history of the player's decisions.

---

# Design North Star

Whenever a new mechanic is added, ask:

1. Does it connect city-building and defense?
2. Does it create interesting choices instead of simple upgrades?
3. Does it support vector synergy?
4. Does it make the city feel like a system?
5. Does it allow unusual strategies?
6. Does it fit the lore of rebuilding after a strange apocalypse?

If the answer is mostly yes, the mechanic belongs.

If not, it should be simplified, removed, or moved to another system.
