# Best Idle Game Ever 2 — Economy

## Purpose of this document

This document describes the economic systems of the game.

The economy is one of the core differentiators of the project.

Unlike traditional city builders or idle games, the player does not primarily accumulate resources.

Instead, the player increases the amount of complexity the city can sustain.

The economy is based around support.

---

# Design Goals

The economy should:

- prevent passive waiting;
- encourage city planning;
- create meaningful tradeoffs;
- naturally generate combat pressure;
- support both Nomad and Colonizer playstyles;
- remain understandable despite having multiple vectors.

---

# Core Economic Concept

Most games use:

Production
↓
Storage
↓
Spending

This game uses:

Production
↓
Support Capacity
↓
Allocation

Resources are not stockpiled forever.

Resources represent the city's ability to maintain systems.

---

# Support

Support is the primary economic resource.

Support is generated by buildings.

Support is consumed by:

- towers;
- buildings;
- wall reinforcement;
- megaprojects;
- special systems.

Support represents ongoing maintenance.

Examples:

Technology:
- electricity
- computing

Medieval:
- people
- administration

Nature:
- biomass
- ecosystems

Aether:
- mana
- magical infrastructure

---

# Why Support Exists

Support solves several design problems.

It prevents:

- infinite hoarding;
- idle waiting;
- exponential stockpiling;
- "come back in a week" gameplay.

Instead the player is rewarded for improving the city itself.

---

# Support Capacity

Each vector generates a maximum support capacity.

Example:

Technology:

Production:
+500 Electricity

Consumption:
-350 Electricity

Free:
150 Electricity

The free amount may be allocated elsewhere.

---

# Produced vs Free Support

This distinction becomes important later.

Produced Support:

Total output of a city.

Free Support:

Unused output.

Many mechanics use one but not the other.

Example:

Specialist migration uses Produced Support.

Capital imports use Free Support.

This prevents exploitation.

---

# Four Economic Vectors

The game contains four partially independent economies.

Strengths:

# Economic Conversion

Late-game technologies allow partial substitution.

Examples:

Food Synthesizer:

Biomass → Electricity

Automation:

People → Electricity

Necromancy:

Mana → People

Bio-reactors:

Biomass → Mana

The goal is not replacement.

The goal is flexibility.

---

# Building Economy

Buildings are economic engines.

Every building has:

Production
Consumption
Threat
Adjacency Effects

Example:

Power Plant

+ Electricity
+ Threat

Farm

+ Biomass
+ Low Threat

Library

+ Ancient Knowledge
- Mana

---

# Adjacency Economy

Buildings should not be evaluated individually.

Neighboring structures matter.

Example:

Farm + Warehouse

Farm production +25%

Laboratory + Power Plant

Computing +20%

Temple + Library

Knowledge +15%

The city should feel like a machine assembled from interacting pieces.

---

# Multistructure Economy

Multistructures are advanced economic districts.

A multistructure is created by upgrading a core building.

Requirements:

- adjacent support buildings;
- specific building types;
- enough support.

Benefits:

- unique effects;
- research unlocks;
- superior efficiency.

Costs:

- increased threat;
- more complexity.

---

# Threat as Economic Pressure

Threat is not a combat mechanic.

Threat is also an economic mechanic.

The city "pays" for growth using threat.

More buildings
↓
More support
↓
More threat
↓
Stronger attacks

Threat acts as a balancing force.

---

# Wall Allocation

Support can be allocated to the wall.

This reduces effective danger.

Tradeoff:

Support spent on the wall cannot be spent elsewhere.

This creates two archetypes:

Strong Economy
Weak Wall

or

Weak Economy
Strong Wall

Both should remain viable.

---

# Deficit System

Support may become negative.

This is intentional.

Example:

Electricity Production:
400

Consumption:
500

Balance:
-100

Consequences:

- no new electric buildings;
- no new electric tower parts;
- existing systems continue functioning.

This models old infrastructure surviving on momentum.

---

# Why Existing Buildings Continue Working

The goal is to create interesting recovery situations.

Without this rule:

Small mistakes instantly destroy a city.

With this rule:

The player can enter debt and recover later.

More interesting.

---

# Specialist Economy

Used by Nomad players.

When abandoning a city:

The player extracts specialists.

Formula:

+1% support bonus
per 100 Produced Support

Only Produced Support matters.

Not Free Support.

This prevents abuse.

---

# Why Specialists Exist

They solve multiple problems:

- make migration meaningful;
- reward successful cities;
- avoid abstract prestige currencies;
- preserve narrative consistency.

Specialists represent accumulated expertise.

---

# Colonizer Economy

Alternative progression path.

Instead of abandoning cities:

The player keeps them alive.

Cities become colonies.

Colonies send support to the capital.

---

# Capital Imports

A colony transfers part of its Free Support.

Example:

Free Electricity:
100

Transfer Rate:
25%

Capital receives:
25

The colony keeps:
75

---

# Why Imports Use Free Support

Using Produced Support creates recursive calculations.

Using Free Support keeps the system simple.

No chains.

No loops.

No bookkeeping nightmare.

---

# Capital Economy

The capital becomes a special city.

Characteristics:

- receives imports;
- supports megaprojects;
- unlocks empire-scale development.

The capital becomes an economic sink.

---

# Capital Deficits

Imports may create shortages.

This is allowed.

A capital can become:

- old;
- inefficient;
- overextended.

Just like real historical capitals.

---

# Capital Transfer

The player may move the capital.

Requirements:

- no import dependency;
- enough local support;
- positive balance after transfer.

This prevents exploitative relocation.

---

# Megaproject Economy

Endings are expensive.

They consume massive support.

Examples:

Temporal Shield

Requires huge electrical infrastructure.

World Tree

Requires enormous biomass.

Ascension

Requires sustained mana.

Empire

Requires people and gold.

The economy ultimately exists to support these projects.

---

# Economic Success

A successful city is not one with the largest numbers.

A successful city is one that can sustain complexity.

The key question should always be:

> Can this city support what I want to build next?

Not:

> How much gold do I have?

---

# Design Rules

Whenever a new economic mechanic is proposed:

1. Does it use support?
2. Does it create allocation decisions?
3. Does it interact with threat?
4. Does it encourage city planning?
5. Does it support at least one progression path?

If not, reconsider adding it.
